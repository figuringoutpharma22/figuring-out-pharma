What I actually learned about pharma during my industrial training — and what nobody tells you

Two months on a GMP manufacturing floor taught me more about the gap between pharma education and pharma reality than three years of textbooks. Here is what genuinely surprised me.

Piyush Singh · June 2026 · 8 min read

When I started my industrial training at a GMP-regulated pharma manufacturer, I had a clear picture of what it would be like. Rows of gleaming equipment, white-coated scientists, everything running with precision. Three years of B.Pharma had given me that mental image.

The reality was more interesting — and more instructive — than anything I had imagined.

What GMP actually means when you are standing inside it

Good Manufacturing Practice is something every pharma student learns in theory. You know the definition. You can write an exam answer about documentation, batch records, and SOPs. What you cannot learn from a textbook is what it feels like to work inside a GMP environment — the constant documentation, the way every decision gets signed and countersigned, the culture of traceability.

Nothing moves without a paper trail. Not a single raw material, not a single process step. I watched experienced operators stop everything — an entire production line — to investigate a number that did not match by a fraction. That is not inefficiency. That is the system working exactly as designed.

The difference between academic GMP and real GMP is the same as the difference between reading about swimming and being in the water. Everything makes more sense once you are in it.

The gap between product quality and market-ready presentation

This was the observation that stayed with me longest. A pharmaceutical product can be technically excellent — meeting every specification, passing every quality check — and still lose in the market to a competitor with inferior chemistry but better packaging and a stronger brand name.

I spent time mapping packaging cost structures against regulatory requirements. What I found was that packaging decisions are not purely aesthetic. They are a negotiation between compliance, cost, shelf life, and brand perception — all happening simultaneously, all with their own constraints.

Key insight

The operations team optimises for compliance and cost. The marketing team optimises for perception and recall. These two objectives are often in direct tension, and nobody in the textbook told me that tension was normal.

The cross-functional reality nobody prepares you for

In college, subjects are siloed. Quality Assurance is a separate paper from Pharmaceutical Marketing. Regulatory Affairs is separate from Production. In an actual manufacturing facility, these departments are in constant conversation — and often in conflict.

QA wants more testing. Production wants faster timelines. Marketing wants packaging changes that QA needs to validate. Regulatory Affairs needs documentation for all of it. Understanding that the real job is managing these interfaces, not just executing within one function — that was the single biggest gap between my education and the professional reality.

What this means if you are still in B.Pharma

Your degree teaches you the science. It does not teach you the business. It teaches you what a batch record is — not why the batch record exists within a system of incentives, regulations, liabilities, and commercial pressures.

The students who move fastest after graduation are not the ones who scored highest on exams. They are the ones who understood early that pharma is a business that happens to be built on science — not a science that happens to sell products.

Go for the industrial training. Ask questions that are not on the syllabus. The manufacturing floor will teach you things that no lecture can.
